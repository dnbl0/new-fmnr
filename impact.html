The evidence

Four decades.
Eight countries.
One clear
conclusion.

This page summarises findings from World Vision Australia's 2019 meta-analysis of eight recently completed FMNR projects spanning Timor-Leste, Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania, Uganda, Ethiopia and Ghana — covering more than 900,000 direct beneficiaries.

Across all key outcome areas — trees, soil, income, food security, child wellbeing and gender equality — project participants consistently outperformed non-participants. The evidence is not anecdotal. It is rigorous, independently evaluated and reproducible.

Source: World Vision Australia, Evidence of Impact: Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration (2019). Meta-analysis of 8 projects, endline surveys n=5,095 households.

01 — Environment

Land & Trees
Restored

FMNR produces rapid, measurable improvements in tree cover, soil fertility and erosion — transforming degraded farmland back into productive, biodiverse landscapes.

3×
Tree density increase in Kenya

In the FMNR East Africa mid-term review, tree density on farms in Kenya rose from approximately 22 to 74 trees per hectare in just three years — a three-fold increase driven entirely by farmer-managed regeneration.

Odwori et al., 2016
6×
Tree density increase in Rwanda

In Rwanda, the same three-year period saw tree density increase from 33 to 198 trees per hectare — a six-fold increase. In Senegal's SFLEI project, density rose from zero to 33 trees per hectare.

Jean and Medard, 2016
+15pp
More likely to report soil improvement

Across all eight projects, participants were 15 percentage points more likely to report improved soil quality and fertility than non-participants — and 10 percentage points more likely to report reduced erosion.

World Vision meta-analysis, 2019

Humbo, Ethiopia: soil vs comparison

The Humbo project is one of the few FMNR studies with a true comparison group. The contrast is stark: in project areas, 83% of participants reported improved soil quality. In comparison areas just 3km away, only 22% reported the same.

For erosion reduction the gap was even larger: 85% of project participants reported reduced erosion, compared to 22% in comparison areas — a 63 percentage point difference attributable to FMNR.

"Before there were no trees, it was bare degraded land and temperatures were very high. After protection, the trees started growing fast, wildlife started coming back, the temperature has gone down, and rainfall is getting better."

— Child members of Environment Club, Humbo, Ethiopia

02 — Economy

Income &
Livelihoods

FMNR generates measurable income gains for participating households — through improved crop yields, wood and forest products, and reduced expenditure on inputs previously purchased from outside the farm.

+23pp
More likely to report income increase

In Kenya, Rwanda and Tanzania, participant households were 22–24 percentage points more likely to report an increase in household income. The effect held equally for women, poorer households and female-headed households.

World Vision meta-analysis, 2019
$72
Average income gain per household

External research across the Sahel found FMNR increased household income by US$72 per year. Wood sales alone generated US$46–92 per household per year. In Mali, the value of restored native tree species reached up to US$650 per household annually.

Binam et al., 2015; Tougiani et al., 2009; Faye et al., 2010
$638K
Carbon credit revenue in 10 years

In Humbo, Ethiopia, sales of certified carbon credits generated US$638,000 over the first decade — funding grain storage, flour mills, microfinance for animals and solar lights, and warehouse construction for the cooperative.

World Vision Ethiopia, 2017

03 — People

Food, Families
& Futures

FMNR's effects ripple beyond the farm: fewer months without food, healthier children, women with more time and agency, and communities with stronger social trust.

−0.26
Months of food shortage per year

Across seven project sites, participants reported significantly fewer months of food shortage per year compared to non-participants. FMNR adopters were also found to have improved dietary diversity and greater capacity to cope with droughts and floods.

World Vision meta-analysis, 2019; Binam et al., 2015a
+8pp
More likely to provide well for children

Participant households were 8 percentage points more likely to be able to provide two sets of clothes, shoes and a blanket for all children. Critically, this effect was 15 percentage points for the poorest households — FMNR benefits children who need it most.

World Vision meta-analysis, 2019
47→18%
Households earning under $100/year

In Timor-Leste's BRACCE project, the proportion of households earning less than US$100 per year fell from 47% to 18% over the project period — a dramatic reduction in extreme poverty attributed to FMNR and associated livelihoods activities.

Anda, 2016 / BRACCE Endline

Women at the centre

By increasing the availability of firewood close to home, FMNR directly reduces one of the most time-consuming burdens on women and girls in the developing world. At endline, 17% of female respondents in East Africa explicitly named decreased workload as a benefit.

The effects extend further: 52% of female respondents reported increased income as a direct project benefit. 24% identified increased involvement in village decision-making. FMNR groups functioned as a conduit for women's participation in community governance — something not explicitly designed into the program.

"Women and children now spend less time collecting firewood and finding grass and fodder for livestock."

— FMNR East Africa Kenya mid-term evaluation, Odwori et al., 2016

In Senegal's SFLEI project, 70% of women reported increased income compared to 61% of men — reflecting the particular opportunity FMNR creates for women to generate income from wood and forest products.

04 — Climate

Carbon &
Sequestration

FMNR is a verified, audited climate solution. Two Ethiopian projects alone have sequestered over 276,000 tonnes of CO₂ — while generating income that funds community infrastructure.

181K
Tonnes CO₂ sequestered — Humbo

The Humbo Ethiopia project is certified to the UN Clean Development Mechanism and externally audited by forestry experts. Since 2007 it has sequestered 181,650 tonnes of CO₂ through community-managed natural regeneration of degraded forest land.

World Vision Ethiopia CDM Report, 2017
94K
Tonnes CO₂ sequestered — Soddo

The Soddo Ethiopia project, certified to the Gold Standard, has sequestered 94,817 tonnes of CO₂ since 2007. Together, Humbo and Soddo represent over 276,000 tonnes of independently verified carbon removal by smallholder farming communities.

World Vision Ethiopia / Gold Standard, 2017
