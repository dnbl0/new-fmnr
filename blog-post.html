How one community in Niger restored 400 hectares using FMNR

On This Page

The beginning The technique Results after five years What’s next

Share

In the Maradi region of southern Niger, a community that once watched its farmland turn to dust has reversed decades of land degradation — pruning and protecting naturally regenerating trees to transform nearly 400 hectares into productive, resilient land.

The beginning

When Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration was first introduced to the village of Guidan Wari in 2018, the community was sceptical. Decades of conventional land clearing had left the soil compacted and vulnerable to erosion. Crop yields had been declining for years, and many families were relying on food aid to survive the dry season.

World Vision's local field team began working with community leaders to protect and selectively prune the native trees and shrubs already germinating from intact root systems beneath the ground. The practice required no seeds, no nurseries, no chemical inputs — just deliberate, sustained observation and restraint.

"At first we thought it was strange — do nothing, let the trees grow, just prune them carefully. But after the first season we could already see the difference in the soil." — Ahmadou Issa, community elder, Guidan Wari

The technique

FMNR involves selecting the most promising stems from each shrub or tree stump, removing competing growth, and allowing the chosen stems to mature. Farmers designate which trees they are responsible for, creating an informal but powerful system of stewardship. Trees are retained on farmland rather than cleared, providing shade for crops, fixing nitrogen in the soil, and creating natural windbreaks.

Training was delivered through practical field sessions led by experienced FMNR practitioners from neighbouring villages. Within three months, more than 80 farmers in Guidan Wari had been trained, and a community bylaw was established to protect regenerating trees from livestock grazing during the growing season.

Results after five years

By 2023, the community had protected and regenerated trees across 387 hectares of previously degraded land. Millet yields increased by an average of 40% in fields where FMNR had been practised for three or more seasons. Families reported significantly less time spent collecting firewood, and several women's groups had begun producing and selling shea nuts and moringa leaves — a new source of income made possible by the returning tree cover.

Groundwater levels also improved, with a shallow well that had run dry in the early 2010s returning to reliable seasonal water. Community members attribute this to the increased soil permeability from root systems and leaf litter cover.

"The land was dying when I was young. Now my children will inherit something living." — Hawa Moussa, smallholder farmer

What’s next

The Guidan Wari community is now supporting three neighbouring villages to begin their own FMNR programmes. A cross-community monitoring group meets quarterly to track tree density, crop yields and soil health using simple field survey methods developed with World Vision.

The local government has included FMNR in its district land management strategy, making this one of the first formally recognised community-led restoration programmes in the Maradi region.
