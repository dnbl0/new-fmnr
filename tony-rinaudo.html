On a mission

Restoring the world's
drylands,

at a time.

Tony Rinaudo arrived in Niger in 1981 as a young Australian agronomist with a mission to fight famine. What he discovered — buried beneath the bare soil — would change the lives of millions of people across the developing world and reshape how the world thinks about land restoration.

By recognising that the stumps and roots of long-suppressed trees still lay alive beneath degraded farmland, Tony pioneered Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration (FMNR): a simple, low-cost technique that empowers farmers to regenerate trees on their own land. No saplings. No trucks. No budget. Just knowledge, persistence, and community.

The journey

Accelerating
land restoration
since 1981

1981 A young agronomist arrives in the Sahel

Tony Rinaudo arrives in the Maradi region of Niger as a missionary agronomist with Serving in Mission (SIM). The landscape is bleak — a semi-arid wasteland stripped bare by decades of over-farming, drought and population pressure. Conventional development approaches, from tree-planting campaigns to large-scale irrigation projects, were failing to reverse the tide.

1983 The underground forest discovery

During a moment of despair on a dusty road, Tony notices small green shoots emerging from what appear to be dead tree stumps. He realises these are not weeds — they are the living remnants of a vast "underground forest" suppressed for generations by farmers who were taught that trees competed with crops.

The insight is revolutionary: the root systems of ancient trees still survive across millions of hectares of degraded farmland. By selectively pruning and protecting these shoots rather than cutting them down, farmers can regenerate productive trees in months — at virtually zero cost. FMNR is born.

1990s Niger's great green wall begins

FMNR spreads across the Maradi and Zinder regions of Niger as farmers witness their neighbours' fields transform. Satellite imagery later confirms what local communities already know: over 5 million hectares of land have been regenerated across southern Niger — one of the largest environmental transformations in African history, driven entirely by smallholder farmers.

Studies document dramatic improvements in soil fertility, crop yields, water retention and biodiversity. Food security improves for an estimated 2.5 million people. The term "The Re-greening of the Sahel" enters the scientific literature.

2000s World Vision and global expansion

Tony joins World Vision Australia as Principal Advisor on Food Security, providing the platform and resources to scale FMNR internationally. Training programs are developed and facilitated across Ethiopia, Mali, Senegal, Burkina Faso and beyond. FMNR begins appearing in the academic literature as a proven, replicable methodology.

The global development community begins to take notice. The World Bank and CGIAR research centres start documenting FMNR outcomes. Tony becomes the leading advocate for farmer-centred, low-cost land restoration at an international level.

2018 Right Livelihood Award — the "Alternative Nobel"

Tony receives the Right Livelihood Award — widely known as the "Alternative Nobel Prize" — in recognition of his transformative work in land restoration and food security. The award acknowledges not just the scale of FMNR's impact but the profound paradigm shift it represents: empowering communities rather than imposing external solutions.

The award elevates FMNR to global prominence, attracting new research, policy attention and funding. Tony uses the platform to advocate for FMNR's inclusion in national and international climate and food security strategies.

Today 26 countries. Millions of hectares. Growing.

FMNR is now practised in 26 countries across Africa, Asia and the Pacific. Tens of millions of hectares of degraded land are in various stages of regeneration. Tony continues to travel, train and advocate — from village meetings in Ethiopia to climate conferences in Geneva — spreading the simple message that the tools for restoration are already in the ground.

His book The Forest Underground brings the story to a global audience. The FMNR Hub network continues to expand the practice with training programs, research partnerships and community engagement at scale.

About

Tony
Rinaudo

Principal Advisor & FMNR Pioneer

Tony grew up in rural Australia with a deep love of the land. After completing his agricultural studies he moved to Niger in 1981 with his wife Liz, not knowing that a chance observation beside a dusty road would set the course for the rest of his life.

Over four decades he has dedicated himself to demonstrating that degraded land is not dead land — and that the communities who live on that land hold the keys to its restoration. His approach is rooted in humility: Tony does not impose solutions, he reveals them.

Today Tony is recognised as one of the world's leading voices on farmer-led land restoration. He is an Honorary Associate Professor at the University of New England, a Right Livelihood Laureate, and author of The Forest Underground.
